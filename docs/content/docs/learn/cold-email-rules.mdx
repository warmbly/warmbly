## Why this matters

Cold email is not illegal in most jurisdictions, but the rules around it vary. Getting them wrong invites both fines and the worse outcome: spam complaints that damage your sender reputation across the entire contact list.

<Callout type="info" title="Not legal advice">
This guide is general information, not legal advice. If you're sending at scale to regulated jurisdictions, run your specific approach past counsel.
</Callout>

## United States: CAN-SPAM (2003)

CAN-SPAM applies to "commercial electronic mail messages". It does *not* require prior consent for cold B2B email. It does require:

- Truthful "From" and routing information.
- Subject lines that aren't misleading.
- Identification as an advertisement (interpreted loosely for B2B context).
- A valid physical postal address.
- A clear unsubscribe mechanism honoured within 10 business days.

For Warmbly's defaults, this means: include a physical address in your sender profile, never spoof a name or domain, and ensure unsubscribe replies are auto-suppressed.

## European Union and UK: GDPR + PECR

GDPR applies to personal data of EU/UK residents. PECR (in the UK) and ePrivacy directives in EU member states specifically cover electronic marketing.

### B2B cold mail

Cold mail to **corporate** recipients (a named role at a company) is generally permitted under "legitimate interest" if:

- The recipient has a clear business reason to receive the message (relevance test).
- You can demonstrate you've considered their rights and interests (balancing test).
- You provide a clear opt-out mechanism.
- You honour opt-outs immediately.
- You can tell them, on request, where you obtained their contact details.

### B2C marketing mail

Generally requires prior consent. Cold marketing to consumer addresses in the EU/UK is risky without it.

### Data processing record

Keep a record of where you sourced each contact and the legitimate interest you relied on. Warmbly's contact import wizard captures source and import notes for exactly this.

## Canada: CASL (2014)

CASL is the strictest of the major regimes. It requires *express or implied consent* before sending commercial electronic messages to Canadian recipients.

Implied consent exists where the recipient has a relevant existing business relationship, conspicuously published their business email without an explicit "no marketing" statement, or asked for information from your business in the last 6 months.

Every message must include sender identification, contact info, and a working unsubscribe. Penalties for non-compliance can reach CAD $10M per violation for organisations.

## Australia: Spam Act 2003

Requires consent (express, inferred or implied), accurate sender identification and a functional unsubscribe. Inferred consent is generous for B2B but not consumer marketing.

## Practical cold-mail compliance checklist

1. Use a real From: name and a domain you actually own.
2. Include a physical mailing address in your sender profile or footer.
3. Provide a clear opt-out, such as "Reply STOP to opt out" or a one-click link.
4. Auto-suppress recipients who opt out, across all future sequences and campaigns.
5. Record where each contact was sourced and your basis for contacting them.
6. Only contact role accounts and named business addresses for B2B; avoid personal addresses unless you have explicit consent.
7. Customise the message for relevance: boilerplate to thousands of strangers is the loudest possible compliance and reputation risk.
8. Skip Canadian and EU consumer-mail recipients unless you have documented consent.
9. Honour opt-outs within 24 hours, not the legal maximum.
10. If you operate a privacy policy, link to it from your footer.
